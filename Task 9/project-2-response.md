## Project 2 Response (to Kaisa Catt's work)
> https://github.com/kaikukuu/CART263-Project1/tree/main
> https://kaikukuu.github.io/CART263-Project1/

Kaisa's project was one of the most emotionally compelling projects I experienced because it uses a small interactive 3D space to talk about something much larger: memory, grief, and the way objects can hold the presence of someone who is no longer there. What surprised me most was that the scene is not built as a complete realistic room. Instead, the room floats in a dark universe, surrounded by stars. This choice makes the space feel incomplete, isolated, and almost dreamlike, which fits what she wrote in her artist’s statement about trying to remember a room that now exists only in fragments. The absence of Grandma herself is also powerful. The work does not try to recreate the person directly; it lets the objects speak for her.

The most interesting part of the experience is how each object works like a memory trigger. The conch shell, record player, rotary phone, camera, book, rocking chair, lamp, and cat all invite the viewer to interact, but the interactions are not just decorative. They create emotional moments. For example, the conch shell connects to the sound of the sea, which becomes both a childhood memory and a comforting message. The camera shows photographs and reminds the viewer that even ordinary moments can become precious memories. The phone interaction is especially strong because it creates anticipation through ringing, waiting, and answering, almost like trying to receive a message from the past. The rocking chair also stood out to me because it emphasizes absence: the chair can move, but the person who should be sitting there is gone.

The project uses Three.js to build the 3D scene, including the camera, lighting, shadows, floating floor, stars, and shooting stars. The code also imports 3D models with GLTFLoader and FBXLoader, then places each object carefully in the room. I found the use of Raycaster particularly important because it allows the viewer to click directly on 3D objects and trigger different memories. The record player cycles through several songs and updates the “Now Playing” display, while the phone uses a more complex state system with waiting, ringing, answering, and voicemail. These details make the project feel less like a static model and more like an interactive memory archive.

One thing I love the most is the combination of 3D space with 2D overlays. When the viewer clicks the book, camera, or conch shell, images and videos appear over the scene. This layering feels appropriate because memory itself is layered: a physical object opens into a photograph, a sound, or a short emotional message.

Overall, I really love this project not only because its technical choices, but also the emotional concept which support each other. The universe setting, the incomplete room, the nostalgic objects, and the interactive sounds all create a quiet experience of remembering someone through what they left behind. What stayed with me most was the idea that forgetting a person’s face does not mean losing them completely; memory can survive in small objects, repeated gestures, and sensory details.
